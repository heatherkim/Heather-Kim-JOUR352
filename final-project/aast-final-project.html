How student activism lead to victory for the Asian American community at UMD


By Heather Kim

Background

Within 49 years, the Asian American community at the University of Maryland, College Park went from being hardly recognized to hardly recognizable. By this, I mean that the Asian American Pacific Islander (AAPI) community has evolved to a point where students from just a few decades ago could hardly hope for.

In 1970, Shirley Hune taught the first Asian American studies course at UMD, however it would not be until 30 years later that the official program for Asian American Studies (AAST) is created.

The AAST program continues to grow at UMD year after year, along with the university’s growing AAPI population. However, what some students may not know is that the AAST program was created as a result of student engagement and activism.

The fight for AAST to be recognized officially started in 1995, according to the AAST’s annual report from this past year. In the next five years that followed, students along with the support of staff members and fellow community leaders, such as members of the Black Student Union, continued to advocate for a new AAST program. The proposal was repeatedly shelved and ignored for years.

The Asian American Student Union logos over time. From left: 2012, 2014, 2016. (2016 Logo by: Charlotte Li. Other logos: Courtesy of AASU)

Students organized protests, sit-ins, teach-ins, high school visits and informational sessions. One specific instance that is cited on the AAST website is when during one protest in front of the Adele H. Stamp Student Union, a leader from the Black Student Union pushed students to march to the Administration building.

During this demonstration a crowd of approximately 200 students began to chant “Asian American Studies Now!” Following this march, staff and student leaders met with the university president to negotiate the program. This became a catalyst for the rest of the movement, leading to the creation of a task force specifically focused on furthering the AAST proposal, and later the approval of the AAST program in 2000.


AAST Over the Years

UMD Asian American/Pacific Islander Undergraduate Enrollment

The number of Asian American Pacific Islander (AAPI) students at UMD has increased at an accelerated rate following a slight dip in 2012. The data includes undergraduate students who identify as Asian American or Pacific Islander. As of 2017, Asian American students made up approximately 20 percent of UMD's undergraduate population, making AAPI students the largest minority group on campus.

Source: Data collected by UMD's Campus Counts, Office of Institutional Research, Planning & Assessment

Just within the last seven years, the AAPI population at UMD has increased at an accelerated rate. Asian American students now make up more than 20 percent of UMD’s campus. This makes Asian Americans the second largest racial group on campus following whites.

Number of Enrolled AAST Minors

The Asian American Studies (AAST) Program was officially approved by the University Senate in 2000, however the minor was not approved until 2007. Within a matter of a few years, the amount of AAST minors has almost tripled in size.

Source: Data collected by The Asian American Studies Program Annual Report

What's Next

Since 2013, the amount of enrolled AAST minors at UMD has more than doubled. This statistic also implicates that the amount of students learning about AAPI issues, advocacy, history and culture has more than doubled in the last few years. In a time where the voices of marginalized communities continue to be underrepresented and ignored, students can look back on the actions of past students and see what raising their voices can do.
